# The Question

For thousands of years, humanity has attempted to answer one question.

> What is a human?

The question appears simple.

It may be the most difficult question we can ask.

---

# An Observation

Throughout history, humans have continually extended themselves through tools.

Stone tools extended physical strength.

Fire extended survival.

Language extended cooperation.

Writing extended memory.

Printing extended knowledge.

Machines extended labor.

Computers extended calculation.

The Internet extended access to information.

Artificial intelligence may extend reasoning.

Each generation delegated abilities that were once inseparable from being human.

If this continues,

what remains?

---

# The Ship of Theseus

Suppose we replace every capability one by one.

Physical strength.

Memory.

Calculation.

Navigation.

Communication.

Reasoning.

Creativity.

Decision making.

At what point do we stop calling the result human?

Or perhaps the question itself is mistaken.

---

# Beginning Without an Answer

Semut does not begin by defining humanity.

Instead, it begins with observation.

History suggests that humans continuously reshape themselves through technology.

Rather than concluding what humanity is, Semut continues asking.

---

# Perspectives

Many traditions have attempted to answer this question.

Examples include philosophy, biology, psychology, anthropology, neuroscience, and artificial intelligence.

Each contributes part of the picture.

None are assumed to provide the final answer.

---

# A Possible Direction

Perhaps humanity is not defined by any single capability.

Not memory.

Not strength.

Not intelligence.

Not language.

Perhaps what matters is something that persists while these capabilities change.

Whether such a thing exists remains an open question.

---

# Why This Matters

Artificial intelligence is often discussed in terms of capability.

Semut asks a different question.

As technology becomes increasingly capable,

what should it preserve?

---

# Open Questions

* What is a human?
* What changes when memory becomes external?
* What changes when reasoning becomes collaborative?
* Can identity survive complete delegation?
* Is consciousness necessary for humanity?
* Does humanity require embodiment?
* What should technology never replace?
* What should technology always protect?

---

This document has no conclusion.

Not because there is none,

but because the question remains open.
